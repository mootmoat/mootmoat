MOOT MOAT: The v17 Workbook  
The First Draft Canvas

The barrier to surviving Round Zero is no longer translating raw exhaust into JSON-LD. The bridge is this Workbook.

You do not need to understand JSON-LD, ATS ontologies, or entity resolution to start. You only need The Source — the direct memory of constraint environments and decisions made under real consequences.

This workbook contains all twelve Depth Signal canvases. Start with whichever one matches your strongest material. One completed canvas over a cup of coffee is your nucleus. Everything else — the schema encoding, the LinkedIn post, the colophon case study, the website deployment — is downstream of what you write here. You start with the scars; the protocol handles the schema.

The full taxonomy and technique descriptions live in the v17 Spec (`MootMoat_v17_Spec.md`).

DEPTH SIGNAL 01: THE PROVENANCE CHAIN  
*(1 of 12 techniques — full taxonomy in the v17 Spec)*

Pick a design feature you consider battle-hardened from your career. It could be a custom lid for a food automation line, a thermal mitigation strategy, or a DFM tradeoff. How many failures is it thick?

The _____________________________ [feature]

in _____________________________ [product/program]

came from ___________________________,

which came from ____________________,

which came from ____________________.

It is _______ mistakes thick. That's why it works.

Drafting Notes:  
The constraint failures go in the blank lines. Do not edit for grammar. Do not sanitize for HR. Write the exact mechanical, thermal, or systemic realities that forced each iteration. When you treat engineering as a forensic discipline, this canvas is where the autopsy starts.

DEPTH SIGNAL 02: THE STRUCTURAL RHYME  
*(2 of 12 techniques — full taxonomy in the v17 Spec)*

Identify a physical constraint domain you have lived in for years. Now name a digital, organizational, or computational domain with the same failure signature — not the same surface behavior, but the same underlying mechanism of failure.

My physical constraint domain: _____________________________

Its characteristic failure mode: _____________________________

The analogue domain: _____________________________

The shared structural property (the invariant that binds both): _____________________________

The shared failure mode (what collapse looks like in both): _____________________________

Drafting Notes:  
The Structural Rhyme is the primary schema mechanism — it feeds directly into the `isomorphicProof` block in the JSON-LD. The Provenance Principle applies: you write this from lived experience first. The LLM formalizes it; it does not generate it. Write until a prompted LLM couldn't have produced what you wrote.

DEPTH SIGNAL 03: THE FORENSIC NUMBER  
*(3 of 12 techniques — full taxonomy in the v17 Spec)*

Name a specific measurement you still remember from a failure event. Not an estimate — the actual number from the instrument.

The measurement: _____________________________

What it came from: _____________________________

What would have happened if it had been 5% different: _____________________________

Drafting Notes:  
Round numbers come from specs. Jagged numbers come from instruments. The delta — 75°C to 22.6°C, not 70°C to 25°C — is the fingerprint. If you remember the number, write the number.

DEPTH SIGNAL 04: THE COMPRESSED WAR STORY  
*(4 of 12 techniques — full taxonomy in the v17 Spec)*

Name a failure event you can tell in one sentence. The sentence that survives after years of iteration — the one you reach for when you have thirty seconds.

The essential sentence: _____________________________

What it leaves out: _____________________________

Why that's the right thing to leave out: _____________________________

Drafting Notes:  
Compression requires mastery. You cannot compress a story you only half-understand. The sentence reveals what you consider load-bearing. Try the structure: "The [component] that [unexpected behavior] during [constraint condition] because [root cause no one saw coming]."

DEPTH SIGNAL 05: THE CONSTRAINT PERSONALITY  
*(5 of 12 techniques — full taxonomy in the v17 Spec)*

Anthropomorphize your primary constraint domain based on how it has specifically surprised you — not how a textbook describes it.

My constraint domain: _____________________________

Its most dangerous personality trait: _____________________________

The event that taught me this: _____________________________

In one sentence, the character of this domain is: _____________________________

Drafting Notes:  
Generic descriptions of constraint domains are neutral. Personality descriptions are personal. Two engineers with thirty years of thermal experience will anthropomorphize it differently based on what it specifically taught them. The character you describe is a cognitive fingerprint.

DEPTH SIGNAL 06: THE DECISION HEURISTIC  
*(6 of 12 techniques — full taxonomy in the v17 Spec)*

Name a rule you follow that you couldn't have learned from a course or textbook. The rule that crystallized from a specific class of failure.

The rule (one sentence): _____________________________

The specific condition under which it applies: _____________________________

The origin event: _____________________________

What would happen if you ignored it: _____________________________

Drafting Notes:  
Heuristics from textbooks are general. Heuristics from failure are specific and often contain an implicit "because I watched someone not do this." The specificity of the exception clause — at the asymptote, at production clamping force, above 35°C — is the fingerprint.

DEPTH SIGNAL 07: THE COUNTERFACTUAL  
*(7 of 12 techniques — full taxonomy in the v17 Spec)*

Name the specific catastrophe that didn't happen because of something you caught. Be specific about the failure mode, the threshold, and the consequence magnitude.

What I caught: _____________________________

What would have happened: _____________________________

The failure mode: _____________________________

The threshold at which it would have triggered: _____________________________

The consequence magnitude: _____________________________

Drafting Notes:  
Generic counterfactuals are vague ("could have been much worse"). Specific counterfactuals prove you understood the system well enough to know exactly what you were preventing. The consequence magnitude — a field recall, an eight-figure liability, a program cancellation — is the load-bearing element.

DEPTH SIGNAL 08: THE THRESHOLD VOCABULARY  
*(8 of 12 techniques — full taxonomy in the v17 Spec)*

Name a term you use for an engineering phenomenon that you've never seen in a textbook. Language you coined or adopted under pressure, for the territory between "working fine" and "about to fail."

The term: _____________________________

What it describes: _____________________________

Where it came from: _____________________________

The first time you needed a name for it: _____________________________

Drafting Notes:  
You cannot generate this vocabulary from a prompt. It came from watching a specific failure pattern enough times to need a name for it. If you've used a term with colleagues that outsiders find strange, that's the term. Write where it came from.

DEPTH SIGNAL 09: THE TOOL AS CHARACTER  
*(9 of 12 techniques — full taxonomy in the v17 Spec)*

Name the tool you reach for when the stakes are highest and time is shortest. Not the most sophisticated option — the one you trust when everything is on the line.

The tool: _____________________________

Why this one and not the more sophisticated option: _____________________________

The crisis where this became clear: _____________________________

What the sophisticated option can't do under pressure: _____________________________

Drafting Notes:  
Tool preference under crisis reveals epistemic style — how you think, not just what you know. "Proficient in SolidWorks" is a skill claim. "The Excel calculator I built in 2003, because I can see every assumption" is a cognitive fingerprint.

DEPTH SIGNAL 10: THE NEGATIVE SPACE EXPERTISE  
*(10 of 12 techniques — full taxonomy in the v17 Spec)*

Name three things you will never do again in your primary constraint domain. For each, name the origin event.

Never #1: _____________________________  
Origin event: _____________________________

Never #2: _____________________________  
Origin event: _____________________________

Never #3: _____________________________  
Origin event: _____________________________

Drafting Notes:  
A curated list of prohibitions is a portrait of a career. Every "never" encodes a failure. The key is the contextual condition — "never spec a TIM without testing at production clamping force" is specific. "Never skip thermal testing" is not. The condition is the fingerprint.

DEPTH SIGNAL 11: THE TEMPORAL SIGNATURE  
*(11 of 12 techniques — full taxonomy in the v17 Spec)*

Name a problem you engage earlier in the project lifecycle than most of your peers. Name the specific stage. Name what taught you that earlier was better.

The problem: _____________________________

The stage where I engage it: _____________________________

The stage where most engineers engage it: _____________________________

What taught me to move earlier: _____________________________

Drafting Notes:  
Engineers who intervene early in certain domains do so because they've watched late intervention fail. "I start worrying about thermal at the napkin sketch stage" contains a full failure history. The specific stage you name is the fingerprint — it reveals your model of how failures develop.

DEPTH SIGNAL 12: THE MENTOR ECHO  
*(12 of 12 techniques — full taxonomy in the v17 Spec)*

Name something a specific person taught you that you still hear before a decision. Name the moment you understood it was true.

What they said (as close to their exact words as you remember): _____________________________

Who said it: _____________________________

The moment you first understood it was true: _____________________________

The decision you still hear it before: _____________________________

Drafting Notes:  
Generic wisdom sounds like a poster. Mentor Echos sound like a voice — they have the rhythm of the person who said it and the specific context where you first understood what they meant. You cannot fabricate a transmission. Write the voice, not the lesson.
